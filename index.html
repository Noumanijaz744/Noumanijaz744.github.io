01 / About

Research shaped by limited data and changing conditions.

I am a Ph.D. researcher in Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Ulsan, South Korea. My work focuses on machine-learning systems that can learn from small labelled datasets and adapt to previously unseen conditions.

My current research studies few-shot and federated methods for network intrusion detection, with particular attention to zero-day attacks, class-incremental learning, cross-domain adaptation, and behaviour-aware traffic representations.

Before this, I worked on few-shot bioacoustic event detection using transductive inference and spectrogram augmentation. I also study signal-level attacks and robust learning for USRP-based human activity recognition.

02 / Research

Four connected research directions.

I design learning methods for situations where labels are scarce, attacks evolve, and data cannot always be centralized.

01

Few-shot intrusion detection

Detecting unseen and zero-day attacks from a small support set using metric-based meta-learning, reusable behaviour components, and refined class prototypes.

  • Zero-day recognition
  • Prototype refinement
  • Behaviour-aware features
02

Federated and cross-domain learning

Training intrusion detectors across IoT and IoMT environments while retaining local data and improving consistency between domain-specific representations.

  • Domain prototypes
  • Contrastive objectives
  • Privacy-aware training
03

Acoustic event intelligence

Recognizing rare animal calls and environmental sounds through few-shot inference, Mel-spectrogram representations, and data augmentation.

  • Bioacoustics
  • Urban sound classification
  • Transductive inference
04

Wireless sensing security

Studying how jamming, replay, spoofing, frequency offset, sample loss, and data poisoning affect USRP-based sensing systems.

  • USRP systems
  • Attack modelling
  • Robust signal learning
